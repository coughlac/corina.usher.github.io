Truple - Elevating the Pair

Introduction

I'm a firm believer in the power of Pairing for all tasks, no matter how small. Even drafting an email can benefit from a second perspective.

In fact, I would even go so far as to say that Software Teaming (Mob Programming) may be my preferred option for use cases such as:

  • Defining the ubiquitous language of the domain model
  • Discussing and documenting architectural designs
  • Creating Architectural Decision Records (ADRs)

1 - a lone developer?

I frequently hear fellow Pair Programmers mention that when there is an odd number of developers on their team — due to holidays, illness, or other reasons — the lone developer is assigned housekeeping tasks to handle on their own.

This always makes me feel uneasy. I dislike the lone developer problem, as I cannot think of many tasks that would warrant a developer to work on itAND not benefit from Pairing on it.

3 - a magic number?

I have been practising Software Teaming for several years now. As an alternative, I get teams to absorb the lone developer into one of our pairs.

I find Pair Programming to be very effective. So, why is it that engagement between three people, in the same team, deteriorates so quickly?

I have found that they suffer from some of the same challenges as Software Mobbing, particularly, a lack of engagement by the participants not actively in the roles of Navigator or Driver.[1][2]

In my experience, the third person just became a third wheel, typically sharing the Navigator role without contributing anything meaningful. In fact, the competition for this role often diminishes its quality, making it less effective than if the third person had simply disengaged completely, distracted by their phone, chat, or other distractions (which I have found to be the other most popular outcome).

Truple - a magic word?

Rather than assigning the lone developer to housekeeping tasks or allowing them to become a third wheel,I’d like to introduce you to my new experiment: a collaborative unit I refer to as a Truple (a nod to our use of 'tuple' in pairing).

What’s the key difference? It’s the rules. More specifically, it's a refinement of roles, bounded by scopes, that allows everyone to remain engaged in their respective responsibilities.

Furthermore, the redefined roles should create a more focused and efficient collaborative process and that should result in improved quality, productivity and team dynamics.

The Truple Dynamic

By clearly defining the roles and distinct role boundaries within a Truple, it creates an environment where each team member can engage effectively with the task at hand, with minimal overlap.

This structure allows for:

  • Focused execution by the Driver
  • Immediate, task-specific guidance from the Navigator
  • Strategic oversight and big-picture thinking from the Observer
Let's look at the roles in more detail.

The Driver

The Driver is the team member actively typing code and controlling the keyboard and mouse.

The scope is confined to the current problem and its immediate context

Their responsibilities include:

  • Thinking about the syntax and writing the actual code
  • communicating their thoughts and actions with the rest of the group as they code

The Navigator

The Navigator observes the driver's work and provides guidance.

The scope is confined to the current problem and its immediate context

Their responsibilities include:

  • Reviewing the code as it's being written
  • Keeping track of potential issues, bugs, or areas for refactoring
  • Communicating their thoughts and actions with the rest of the group as they guide the Driver
  • Refactors, improvements, and alternative approaches, within the scope of the Navigator

The Observer

This role has been elevated to a more strategic position. The observer is the team member actively taking a broader view, considering how the current changes might impact the system as a whole.

The scope extends beyond the current task and its immediate context to encompass the entire system.

Their responsibilities include:

  • Identifying potential needs for abstraction, within the scope of the Observer
  • Refactoring opportunities, within the scope of the Observer
  • Architectural implications

This refined Truple dynamic, re-balances the responsibilities of the traditional roles amongst the group. In doing so, it leverages the strengths of each team member while maintaining clear lines of responsibility, potentially leading to a more engaged, efficient and thoughtful development processes.

Preliminary Findings:

It is worth saying, that the discipline required in Pairing around role adherence and role rotation are still required here.

So far, in my experiments with this approach, I find the role segregation definitely engages members more than the Third Wheel approach.

  • The Driver experience is improved as it is only the Navigator that is focusing on their guidance.
  • The Navigator is also more effective as their scope is more focused
  • The role of the Observer does mean that their input to the Truple needs to be handled with care, so as not to overly disrupt the flow or introduce too many context shifts for the Navigator and Driver. Communication here is key.

Next Steps

The timing of when the Observer shares their findings or thoughts and how that impacts the flow of the session is key to the success of a Truple session. This can depend on the dynamic of the members or be as personal as to be tailored to 'how you prefer to process information'. Do you like to hear it and let your subconscious get to work immediately? Do you prefer to finish your train of thought before you get interrupted? Something else? What works best for all 3 of you?

Timing of Observations

Knowing when to introduce changes or suggestions is crucial for maintaining workflow. It is best to agree the preferred timings before the start of the session. A lot of this will come down to your own judgement, the people in your current Truple, agreed ways of working for the team and practise.

Possible suggestions could be:

  • Commit Time: when the code is being reviewed and before the narrower scope refactor
  • Prior to starting the task: As Kent Beck philosophises: make the change easy, then make the easy change.

Balancing Context Shifts and Flow

To manage the balance between micro context shifts and maintaining productivity:

  • Regular Check-ins: Schedule brief, periodic discussions to address potential changes without disrupting flow.
  • Clear Communication: Ensure all team members understand when and how suggestions will be incorporated.
  • Flexibility: Be ready to adjust roles and timing based on team needs and project requirements.

What next?

Truple: The new Pair Programming?

The concept of a Truple takes on an intriguing new dimension when we think of AI as a collaborator. Perhaps, the Driver is the AI collaborator, and the Pair assume the roles of Navigator and Observer? A scenario where the AI model assumes the role of the Driver, generating code or content based on prompts and instructions. The developer at the keyboard becomes the Navigator, guiding the AI's output, refining prompts, and making real-time decisions on the generated content. Meanwhile, the traditional Navigator steps into the Observer role, maintaining a broader perspective on the task at hand. Or alternatively, the AI collaborator is let loose in the codebase, to act as the Observer and see if it can suggest anything at a macro level? It may not be quite at that stage yet, but soon perhaps? An experiment for the future

Conclusion

The Truple approach extends the benefits of Pair Programming to a slightly larger group, fostering an inclusive environment, enhancing perspectives, and providing more opportunities for knowledge sharing and skill development. We maintain our commitment to collaborative development while ensuring no team member is redundant or doing mundane tasks by themselves.
