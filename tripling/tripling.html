Triple - Elevating the Pair

I hear pair programmers frequently mention that when there is an odd number of developers on their team — due to holidays, illness, or other reasons — the lone developer is assigned to housekeeping tasks or similar. This always makes me feel uneasy.

I dislike the lone developer problem, as I cannot think of many tasks that would warrant a developer to work on it AND not benefit from pairing on it.

I'm a firm believer in the power of pairing for all tasks, no matter how small. Even drafting an email can benefit from a second perspective.

So, what is the alternative?

3 - a magic number?

How about absorbing the 'lone developer' into one of our pairs? Is it that simple?

Unfortunately not. Simply adding a third person to the pair, without modifying any other aspect of the pairing process, doesn't solve the problem.

In fact, this approach encounters similar issues to Software Teaming (Mob Programming), particularly the challenge of keeping all participants engaged. The person who is neither the Navigator nor the Driver often struggles to remain actively involved in the process.

I find Pair Programming to be very effective. So, why is it that the engagement level between three people, in the same team, deteriorates so quickly?

What was going on?

The discipline of frequent role rotations and adhering to defined roles is much harder with the introduction of a third person within a two role process.

In my experience, the third person became a third wheel, typically sharing the Navigator role, without adding meaningful contribution. In fact, the competition for this role often diminished its quality, making it less effective than if the third person had simply disengaged completely, distracted by their phone, chat, or other distractions (another frequent observation).

So what now? How do we solve the lone developer problem without introducing the third wheel dynamic?

Triple - a magic word?

I’d like to introduce you to a collaborative unit I refer to as a Triple (a nod to our use of 'Tuple' in pairing).

What’s the key difference? It’s the adaptation of the traditional pairing process to encompass threepeople.

More specifically, it's a refinement of roles, bounded by scopes, that allows everyone to remain engaged in their respective responsibilities.

The redefined roles create a more focused and efficient collaborative process that results in improved quality, productivity and team dynamics when working in a group of three.

The Triple Dynamic

By clearly defining the roles and distinct role boundaries within a Triple, it eliminates the tussle between three people for two roles. Instead, it creates an environment where each team member can purposefully engage with the task at hand.

This structure allows for:

  • Focused execution by the Driver
  • Immediate, task-specific guidance from the Navigator
  • Strategic oversight and big-picture thinking from the Observer

Let's look at the roles in more detail.

The Driver

The Driver is the team member actively typing code and controlling the keyboard and mouse.

The scope is confined to the current problem and its immediate context. The driver focuses on the current task at hand, ignoring larger issues for the moment.

Their responsibilities include:

  • Thinking about the syntax and writing the actual code
  • Translating the Navigator's instructions and guidance into code
  • Communicating their thoughts and actions with the rest of the Triple as they code

The Navigator

The Navigator observes the driver's work and provides guidance.

The scope is confined to the current problem and its immediate context

Their responsibilities include:

  • Actively review the code as it's being written, looking for bugs, syntax errors, and potential improvements within the scope of the current problem and its immediate context
  • Keeping track of potential issues, bugs, or areas for refactoring
  • Communicating their thoughts and actions with the rest of the Triple as they guide the Driver

The Observer

This role has been elevated to a more strategic position, focusing on strategic refactoring, architectural alignment, and efficiency improvements beyond the immediate task.

The scope extends beyond the current task and its immediate context to encompass the potential impact of the changes on the entire system. This broad perspective means that the scope is not just looking at the impact of the change from a technical perspective, but also looking at the workflows and processes involved for efficiency improvements.

Their responsibilities include:

  • Using their broader system perspective on the task at hand to identify opportunities for refactoring, or introducing abstractions that allow reuse in other areas of the system, simplifying the overall system
  • Ensuring that the solution aligns with team's architectural goals and decisions, ensuring new Architectural Design Decision (ADRs) are recorded as necessary.
  • Identify opportunities for the team to improve efficiency, including tasks suitable for automation and processes that may need to be challenged or re-evaluated.

Preliminary Findings:

So far, I find the Triple approach more effective than the Third Wheel approach.

The Triple re-balances the responsibilities of the traditional pairing roles amongst the group. In doing so, it leverages the inclusion of the additional person while maintaining clear lines of responsibility. This leads to a more engaged, efficient and thoughtful development processes.

The Driver experience is on a par with that of pairing as the Navigator is focusing on their guidance. The Navigator role is arguably more effective as their scope is more focused.

The role of the Observer does mean that their input to the Triple needs to be handled with care, so as not to overly disrupt the flow or introduce too many context shifts for the Navigator and Driver. Communication here is key.

Next Steps

Timing of Observations

Knowing when to share the findings of the Observer is crucial for maintaining workflow and balancing context shifts within the Triple. I think the next steps will be to explore the answer to the question:

What are the optimal times to feed the Observer's findings back into the Triple.

Possible suggestions could be:

  • Commit Time: when the code is being reviewed and before the narrower scope refactor
  • Prior to starting the task: As Kent Beck says: make the change easy, then make the easy change.

Maybe it will come down to an agreement with the people in your current Triple or agreed ways of working for the team?

Or maybe it will be more tailored to 'how you prefer to process information'. Do you like to hear it and let your subconscious get to work immediately? Do you prefer to finish your train of thought before you get interrupted?

It was suggested to me that perhaps the role of the Observer should also include observing the Triple session flow. Beyond ensuring that timely breaks are scheduled to maintain productivity and that roles are rotated to maintain fresh perspectives and engagement, they could interject their observations into the session at an appropriate time? I like the idea of this approach as it tethers the more 'meta' role of the observer back to the focus of rest of the Triple. I think I will try that out next and see how it works out.

The Future?

Triple: The new Pair Programming?

The concept of a Triple takes on an intriguing new dimension when we think of AI as a collaborator. Perhaps, the Driver is the AI collaborator, and the Pair assume the roles of Navigator and Observer?

A scenario where the AI model assumes the role of the Driver, generating code or content based on prompts and instructions. The developer at the keyboard becomes the Navigator, guiding the AI's output, refining prompts, and making real-time decisions on the generated content. Meanwhile, the traditional Navigator steps into the Observer role, maintaining a broader perspective on the task at hand.

Or alternatively, the AI collaborator is let loose in the codebase, to act as the Observer and see if it can suggest anything at a macro level?

Now, I truly am getting carried away!

Conclusion

The Triple approach extends the benefits of Pair Programming to a slightly larger group, fostering an inclusive environment, enhancing perspectives, and providing more opportunities for knowledge sharing and skill development.

It maintains our commitment to a collaborative development while ensuring no team member is redundant or doing mundane tasks by themselves.
